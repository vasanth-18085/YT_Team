## FLUFF CRITERIA (Cut anything that matches)

| Type | Example | Action |
|------|---------|--------|
| Transition filler | "So, as I was saying..." / "Moving on..." | Cut |
| Redundant restatement | Saying the same thing two different ways | Keep the sharper version, cut the other |
| Hedged opinions | "I feel like..." / "It sort of seems like..." | Rephrase to direct or cut |
| Unnecessary setup | "Before I get into that, let me just say..." | Cut the setup, start the thing |
| Audience flattery | "Great question!" / "If you're smart, you already know..." | Cut |
| Dead-end sentences | Sentences that don't add information or energy | Cut |

My name is Vasanth. I'm a software engineer with an ML background. And I'm teaching myself quant finance — while working full time — in public. This channel is where I document the whole thing.

When I started looking into quant finance, I hit a wall. The advanced tutorials assumed I already knew the field. The finance channels didn't touch code. The Python tutorials didn't explain why you'd use any of it in markets. There was no channel for someone in my position — a software engineer who's curious about quant finance but starting from zero. So I made one.

**Year 1:** I'm doing the CPF — the Certificate in Python and Finance from QuantInsti. I'm also doing EPAT, which is their more advanced quant trading program. And I'm building my math foundations — linear algebra, probability, stochastic processes. CPF and EPAT are real credentials that quant firms recognize. They're structured paths through material that actually matters. I'll also build two projects this year. The first: a market data engine — a system that pulls, cleans, and stores financial data.

**Year 2:** The MScFE — the Master's in Financial Engineering from World Quant University. Fully online and free. It goes deep — derivatives pricing, portfolio optimization, econometrics. And more sophisticated projects: a backtesting framework, a risk model. Real systems, documented publicly.

**Year 3:** Contributing — open source tools, research papers. And working in a quant role. Quant developer, quant researcher — somewhere the ML and finance worlds actually meet. That's the plan. Three years. Documented here.

If you're an ML or software engineer who keeps looking at quant finance and thinking "that's where I want to be" — this channel is for you. If you're working full-time and studying something hard in the hours you can find — this channel is for you. If you want to watch someone figure it out in real time, not just teach from a position of already knowing it — this channel is for you.

I'm not going to tell you about trading strategies that print money. I don't have those. I'm not going to pretend I understand something when I don't. If I'm lost, I'll tell you. This is a learning channel. Not a guru channel. The difference matters.

Concept explainers — I'll take hard quant finance concepts and explain them the way an engineer would. Intuition first. Math second. Code when it matters. Build-in-public videos — I'll document the projects I'm building. Real code. Real decisions. What went wrong and why. Milestone updates — when I pass exams or finish a project, you'll hear about it honestly. And livestreams — study-with-me and build sessions. Watch in real time.

The Quant Noob — that's not ironic. That's accurate. I am a noob in this field. Right now. The interesting part isn't being a noob. The interesting part is documenting what it takes to become less of one — so the next person in the same position has a real reference point.

I don't know if this 3-year plan will work exactly as written. Probably it won't. I don't know how hard the math will get. I can do linear algebra. I've done probability in my ML work. But stochastic calculus — which comes in Year 2 — is territory I haven't touched. The worst outcome of this whole plan: I become a better engineer and understand financial markets far better than I do today. That's not a bad outcome.
