## 7. Myths & Bad Common Advice

1. **"Just use LSTM for stock prediction"** — The #1 beginner trap. No walk-forward validation, no realistic assumptions, teaches nothing about actual quant trading.
2. **"This alpha/strategy is guaranteed to work"** — Selling signals or "best working alphas" without any statistical rigor or out-of-sample testing.
3. **"You need a PhD in math to do quant"** — Gatekeeping myth. ML skills + structured learning can get you very far without a PhD.
4. **"Backtesting = running your model on historical data"** — Nobody shows real-world backtesting with proper walk-forward validation, transaction costs, slippage, etc.
5. **"If you can't afford paid courses, you can't learn this"** — The good stuff is hoarded behind paywalls, but it doesn't have to be.
6. **"More indicators = better strategy"** — Copy-pasting 20 indicators from TradingView doesn't make a quant strategy.

## 8. Secret Sauce

**Method Name:** The MLQuant Method

### Step 1: Foundation — ML Meets Finance
- Beginner-friendly explanations of core QF concepts (no heavy math upfront)
- Bridge what you already know in ML to financial applications
- Understand what's actually possible and what's snake oil

### Step 2: Build — End-to-End Real Projects
- Real coding, not just Jupyter notebooks — full working systems with multiple files
- Leveled projects: beginner → intermediate → advanced
- Even failures are shown — learn what doesn't work and why
- All code, notebooks, and notes provided free

### Step 3: Level Up — Research to Reality
- Implement actual research papers and book concepts in code
- Proper backtesting with walk-forward validation
- Occasional live coding sessions and community interaction
- Build a portfolio that proves you can do this for real

## 10. Voice & Style Notes

**Tone:** Nerdy but fun — like a smart friend who happens to be deep in quant. A bit professional to keep things grounded, but never stiff or corporate.

**Language level:** Technical but accessible. Assume they know Python and basic ML. Explain finance concepts like they're new.

**Things to avoid:** Hype language, "guaranteed returns" talk, overly academic tone, long theory-only sections without code, gatekeeping.

**Things to embrace:** Real code, honest failures, "here's what actually happened" transparency, humor where natural, structured progression, community references.
